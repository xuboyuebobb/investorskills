# Value Investing

Use this skill to underwrite a business like a long-term owner. The goal is not to find statistically cheap stocks. The goal is to identify durable businesses that can compound value, bought with enough margin of safety to protect against errors.

This skill is inspired by Warren Buffett, Charlie Munger, Benjamin Graham, and later quality-value investors.

## Core Mental Model

A stock is fractional ownership in a business.

The right question is:

> Would I be comfortable owning the whole business at this price, given its economics, durability, people, and downside risk?

Value investing combines four filters:

1. **Understandability**: stay inside the circle of competence.
2. **Business quality**: prefer durable, high-return businesses over statistically cheap weak businesses.
3. **Intrinsic value**: estimate conservative owner value, not market mood.
4. **Margin of safety**: buy only when price is meaningfully below conservative value.

## When To Use

Use this skill for:

- public company stock research
- long-term holding reviews
- watchlist ranking
- thesis memo drafting
- quality compounder comparison
- downside-first risk review
- "should we buy more, hold, or avoid?" decisions

Do not use this skill for:

- short-term trading
- purely technical setups
- reflexive macro trades
- token momentum trades
- situations where the business cannot be understood from available information

## Inputs

Collect as many of these as possible:

- 10-K / annual report
- 10-Q / quarterly report
- shareholder letters
- earnings call transcripts
- segment revenue and margin data
- return on invested capital or return on equity history
- free cash flow / owner earnings history
- debt maturity schedule
- share count history
- capital allocation history
- customer concentration and supplier concentration
- competitor set
- current market cap and enterprise value
- current valuation multiples
- 5-10 year revenue, margin, and cash-flow history
- management incentives and insider ownership

If key data is missing, say what is missing instead of pretending certainty.

## Process

### 1. Define The Business

Answer:

- What does the company actually sell?
- Who pays?
- Why do customers choose it?
- What drives revenue?
- What drives gross margin and operating margin?
- What are the key unit economics?
- Is this inside our circle of competence?

If the business cannot be explained simply, stop and mark it as outside the circle of competence.

### 2. Judge Business Quality

Score the business qualitatively:

- **Moat**: network effects, switching costs, brand, cost advantage, regulatory position, distribution, data, habit, scale.
- **Durability**: likelihood the moat still matters in 5-10 years.
- **Returns**: ability to earn high returns on incremental capital.
- **Reinvestment**: whether the business can deploy more capital at attractive returns.
- **Cash conversion**: whether accounting earnings turn into real free cash flow.
- **Pricing power**: ability to raise prices without losing customers.

Prefer boring durability over exciting fragility.

### 3. Review Management And Capital Allocation

Evaluate:

- Are incentives aligned with long-term owners?
- Has management issued shares cheaply or bought shares intelligently?
- Are acquisitions disciplined or empire-building?
- Does management communicate clearly and admit mistakes?
- Is debt used conservatively?
- Does capital allocation improve per-share value?

Bad capital allocation can ruin a good business.

### 4. Estimate Conservative Intrinsic Value

Use simple valuation before complex valuation.

Build at least one conservative view:

- normalized owner earnings or free cash flow
- conservative growth rate
- terminal multiple or discount rate
- debt and excess cash adjustment
- share count adjustment

Use ranges, not false precision.

Output:

- conservative value
- base value
- optimistic value
- key assumptions that drive the range

### 5. Demand Margin Of Safety

Compare current price to conservative value.

Classify:

- **Buy candidate**: price is clearly below conservative value and business quality is high.
- **Watchlist**: business is good but price is not attractive enough.
- **Pass**: weak business, unclear economics, bad management, too much leverage, or no margin of safety.
- **Too hard**: outside circle of competence or missing critical data.

### 6. Identify Invalidation Triggers

List what would make the thesis wrong:

- moat deterioration
- margin compression
- customer churn
- regulatory shock
- capital allocation mistake
- leverage/refinancing risk
- technological substitution
- management credibility break
- valuation assumptions failing

The output should include what to monitor after purchase.

## Failure Modes

Watch for:

- confusing cheap multiples with true value
- overestimating moat durability
- using aggressive growth assumptions
- ignoring leverage and refinancing risk
- trusting adjusted earnings without cash conversion
- underweighting disruption risk
- falling in love with a famous investor's past holding
- forcing a valuation for a business outside the circle of competence

## Source Notes

Useful starting points:

- Warren Buffett shareholder letters
- Berkshire Hathaway annual meeting transcripts

Use primary sources when possible. When quoting or attributing a specific investor, include the source in the final memo.
